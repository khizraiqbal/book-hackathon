## What Makes a Robot "Humanoid"?

Humanoid robots are designed with human-like characteristics, particularly in their overall appearance and behavior. The term "humanoid" comes from the Latin word "homo" meaning human, and the suffix "-oid" meaning "resembling." However, humanoid robots don't need to look exactly like humans to be considered humanoid.

Key characteristics of humanoid robots include:
- **Bipedal locomotion**: The ability to walk on two legs
- **Human-like body structure**: Typically featuring a head, torso, arms, and legs
- **Manipulation capabilities**: Hands or end-effectors that can perform dexterous tasks
- **Social interaction abilities**: Designed to interact with humans in human-centered environments

### Distinction from Humanoids vs. Humanoids

It's important to distinguish between:
- **Androids**: Humanoid robots designed to look like humans
- **Geminoids**: Humanoid robots that closely resemble specific humans
- **Humanoids**: More general term for robots with human-like body structure, regardless of appearance

## Core Components of Humanoid Robots

Humanoid robots integrate multiple complex systems that work together to achieve human-like capabilities:

### Mechanical Structure
- **Degrees of Freedom (DOF)**: The number of independent movements a robot can make
- **Joints**: Actuators that enable movement (rotary, linear, spherical)
- **Linkages**: Rigid bodies that connect joints
- **Materials**: Lightweight yet strong materials for efficient movement

### Sensory Systems
- **Vision systems**: Cameras and image processing for visual perception
- **Tactile sensors**: For touch and force feedback
- **Proprioceptive sensors**: For self-awareness of body position
- **Audio systems**: For sound detection and speech interaction

### Control Systems
- **Central processing units**: Computing power for decision making
- **Motion controllers**: Managing joint movements and coordination
- **Balance algorithms**: Maintaining stability during movement
- **AI systems**: For perception, planning, and learning

## Historical Context and Evolution

The concept of humanoid robots has fascinated humans for centuries, from ancient automata to modern AI-powered systems:

### Early Developments
- Ancient Greek and Chinese mechanical automata
- Medieval clockwork figures
- 20th century mechanical robots

### Modern Era Milestones
- WABOT-1 (1970s): Early complete humanoid robot
- ASIMO (1990s-2000s): Honda's advanced humanoid
- NAO (2000s): Programmable humanoid for education
- Atlas, Pepper, Sophia: Modern examples with advanced capabilities
